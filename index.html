Framing

The project I am developing is an interactive and expressive website that sits between a drawing tool and a sound experiment. I do not want it to function as a professional tool like Photoshop or Procreate. Instead, it is intended to be an open space where users can interact freely in their own way.

From my experiments, I found that adding sound makes the interaction feel more immersive and emotional. It transforms drawing into a more engaging and personal experience.

Reflection on Peer Feedback

Reading through the peer feedback, I was pretty happy by how positive the overall response was. Most users described the experience as playful, calming, and immersive, and many mentioned that their drawing style actually changed after hearing the sound — which, for me, was one of the clearest signs that what I was trying to achieve. What stood out most was that users felt like they were actively playing or controlling the sound through their strokes rather than just passively listening. That sense of agency was something I had aimed for from the very beginning, so seeing it reflected in the feedback felt like a real confirmation that the core idea was working.

Sound design turned out to be one of the most revealing areas of feedback. The flute was the clear favourite, with users consistently describing it as calming, smooth, and well-suited to the drawing experience. The piano, on the other hand, was sometimes seen as too sharp or abrupt, making the overall atmosphere not as gentle and ambient then expected. Therefore in the future, I plan to expand the instrument library by adding more softer, more ambient options like strings, soft synth pads, or gentle woodwinds.

Something I found particularly encouraging was how users responded to the idea of more depth. Rather than feeling like criticism, the recurring requests for more instruments, brush types, and control over sound felt like genuine curiosity. Like people wanted to keep exploring, which told me the concept was engaging enough to support that kind of expansion. Several users also suggested connecting visual properties like brush colour, size, and opacity directly to the sound output, rather than relying only on position across the canvas. That felt like one of the most valuable pieces of feedback I received, because it pointed toward something I want to explore. To act on this in the future, I plan to add a range of new brush types, where switching brushes also changes the sound character. As an example, a soft brush producing a gentle, airy tone while a solid brush triggers something more layered. I might also explore having brush size influence volume or intensity, so drawing with a larger brush feels noticeably different from using a fine one.

Furthermore, a few users pointed out that the "Start Sound" button wasn't obvious when they first opened the page, which I thought so too when standing in the users point of view. Others thought that the sound mapping felt uneven near the top and bottom of the canvas, so I will also need to do small changes on this in the future. Another smaller suggestions were adding a redo function, refining the sound toggle and changing the cursor while drawing, to make a a more polished experience and allow it to feel more considered and complete. Therefore, I plan to add both an eraser tool and a redo button to give users more control and freedom while they work.

Alongside the changes already mentioned, one idea that influenced me and I want to try out is a loop or record function, where the canvas captures the user's brush strokes and replays them back as a audio-visual loop. This would allow users layer their marks and hear them cycle back with sound, almost like building a personal composition. But I haven't done much will looping before, so it'll be a challenge for me. Overall, the feedback I received gave me loads of confidence in the direction of the project and I'm really excited to move on and make this my project look and feel even better!

Conceptual Response

My project responds to the brief by creating an interactive experience centred on exploration rather than outcome. Instead of designing a tool for productivity or polished results, I wanted to build a calm, open-ended space where users can draw freely while engaging with sound at the same time.

The core concept treats simple gestures as something expressive, where drawing becomes a way of “playing” music rather than just making marks on a screen. This was influenced by works like WigglyPaint and the Kandinsky experiment, where user input creates playful and responsive outputs.

By removing goals and strict instructions, I wanted users to discover the interaction through curiosity and trial and error. Sound acts as a layer of feedback that makes each stroke feel more alive and immediate, turning a simple visual activity into something more immersive and sensory.

Technical Approach

My project is built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, and is hosted through GitHub. For the drawing functionality, I used Konva.js to handle canvas interactions, allowing users to draw lines in real time, adjust brush size and colour, and use functions such as undo and clear.

For audio, I used Tone.js to generate and control sound based on user input. The vertical position of each stroke is mapped to musical notes, while the speed of movement influences how strongly the notes are played. This creates a direct relationship between how someone draws and what they hear.

Sound only plays while the user is actively drawing, which helps keep the experience calm and prevents it from becoming overwhelming. The piano and flute instruments are created through different synthesiser settings in Tone.js, giving users a choice in the mood of the interaction.

Overall, the technical focus was on keeping the system lightweight, responsive, and easy to understand, so the connection between gesture, drawing, and sound feels immediate.
